# Day Trip: Sagunto (Sagunt) from Valencia

> **Best for:** History lovers, culture buffs, nature walkers, budget travellers
> **Duration:** Full day recommended (5-7 hours), can be done as a half-day (4 hours minimum)
> **Distance from Valencia:** ~27 km north (~30 minutes by train)
> **Best time:** Late March is ideal -- mild weather, orange groves in bloom, perfect for hillside walking

---

## 1. Why Sagunto? -- History & Significance

Sagunto (Valencian: Sagunt) is one of the most historically layered towns on the Mediterranean coast of Spain. Built along and atop a long ridge overlooking the coastal plain and the sea, it has been continuously inhabited for over 2,500 years. Few towns of its size offer such a dense accumulation of civilizations.

### The Layers of History

**Iberian Origins (6th century BC):** The settlement was originally called **Arse**, founded by the Edetani, an Iberian tribe. They built it on the western portion of the hilltop acropolis, taking advantage of the natural defensive position. Arse was prosperous enough to mint its own bronze and silver coins -- a mark of considerable economic sophistication for the era.

**Greek & Phoenician Connections:** The town had trading links with Greek colonists and Phoenician merchants. Classical writers sometimes attributed its founding to Greeks from Zakynthos (hence the Latinised name Saguntum), though this is considered legendary.

**The Siege That Changed History (219 BC):** This is the event that made Sagunto famous forever. The city had allied itself with Rome, creating a flashpoint with the expanding Carthaginian empire in Iberia. In 219 BC, **Hannibal Barca besieged Saguntum for eight gruelling months**. The Saguntines fought desperately -- they famously wounded Hannibal himself with a javelin drenched in flaming pitch (*falarica*). When all was lost, the defenders chose mass suicide, burning the city rather than surrendering. Rome's failure to aid its ally became the casus belli for the **Second Punic War** -- one of the most consequential conflicts in ancient history. In a real sense, the fate of the Roman Republic was decided on this hilltop.

**Roman Saguntum (2nd century BC - 5th century AD):** After Rome reconquered Spain, Saguntum was rebuilt and flourished. Under Augustus, it received the status of *municipium* and a grand **Forum** was built on the acropolis. Under Tiberius (early 1st century AD), the magnificent **Roman Theatre** was constructed into the hillside, seating 4,000 spectators. The town grew to over 19 hectares and became a significant wine-producing centre, exporting across the Mediterranean. An amphitheatre and circus were also built.

**Visigothic & Moorish Periods (5th-13th century):** After invasions by Alans, Vandals, and Visigoths, the town came under Muslim rule in the 8th century. The Moors called it **Morbiter** (or Murviedro, from Latin *muri veteres* -- "old walls") and reinforced the acropolis fortifications substantially in the 11th century. Uniquely for a town under Muslim rule, Sagunto **maintained a Jewish community**, whose quarter (*juderia*) survives to this day.

**The Reconquista:** El Cid briefly captured the town in 1098. King James I (Jaume I) of Aragon completed the reconquest in the 13th century, integrating it into the Kingdom of Valencia.

**The Name Restored:** The town was known as Murviedro for centuries until the 19th century, when it officially reclaimed its ancient Roman name, Sagunto.

**What Makes Sagunto Special:** Unlike many Spanish historical towns that represent one or two eras, Sagunto offers a visible, walkable timeline from the Iberians through the Romans, Moors, Jews, and medieval Christians -- all compressed into a single hilltop and the streets below it. The castle alone spans nearly 1 km and contains remains from Romans, Visigoths, Arabs, and Christians side by side.

## 3. Attractions -- Complete Guide

### 3.1 Roman Theatre (Teatro Romano) -- The Star Attraction

Spain's **first building ever declared a National Monument** (1896), making it one of the most historically significant structures in the country.

**History:** Built in the first half of the 1st century AD during the reign of Tiberius, carved directly into the northern slope of the castle hill. It originally seated approximately **4,000 spectators** and was used for theatrical performances, public gatherings, and civic events. Over the centuries, much of the stone was pillaged for other buildings. A controversial modern restoration in 1994 by architects Giorgio Grassi and Manuel Portaceli added modern stone and steel elements to reconstruct the stage building (*scaenae frons*) and some seating sections. This sparked a fierce debate about restoration vs. preservation, and parts of the restoration were later partially reversed by court order.

**What you see:** The semi-circular *cavea* (seating area) carved into the rock, the restored stage area, excellent acoustics, and panoramic views over the town and coastal plain below. The theatre is still used for the annual **Sagunt a Escena** theatre and music festival (summer).

### 3.2 Sagunto Castle (Castillo de Sagunto) -- The Hilltop Fortress

A sprawling fortress complex stretching nearly **1 km along the hilltop ridge** above the town. Declared a National Monument in 1931.

**History:** Fortified continuously for over 2,000 years by every civilization that ruled here -- Iberians, Romans, Visigoths, Moors, and medieval Christians all left their mark. The result is a palimpsest of defensive architecture unlike almost anywhere else in Spain.

**What you see:** The castle is divided into **seven distinct plazas** (sections), each representing different eras of construction:

- **Plaza de Armas:** The main nucleus, containing remains of the Roman Forum -- including foundations of a temple, curia (senate house), basilica, a large rectangular cistern, and a row of small shops. The **Epigraphic Museum** (Museo Epigrafico) here displays Roman, Iberian, and Hebrew inscriptions found on the site
- **Plaza de la Ciudadela:** The highest point, with the best panoramic views
- **Plaza de Estudiantes, Plaza de la Conejera, Plaza de Dos de Mayo, Plaza de San Fernando, Plaza de la Almenara:** Additional sections with walls, towers, and gateways from various periods
- **Moorish walls and towers** (11th century) are particularly well-preserved
- Spectacular **360-degree views** over the Mediterranean, the orange groves of the coastal plain, the Sierra Calderona mountains, and Puerto de Sagunto

**Practical details:**
| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Admission** | Free |
| **Opening hours (winter/spring)** | Tuesday-Saturday: 10:00-18:00; Sunday & holidays: 10:00-14:00 |
| **Opening hours (summer, Apr-Oct)** | Tuesday-Saturday: 10:00-20:00; Sunday & holidays: 10:00-14:00 |
| **Closed** | Mondays |
| **Time needed** | 1-2 hours (it is large, allow time to explore) |
| **Access** | Steep uphill walk from the old town via Subida del Castillo (~15-20 min). Wear comfortable shoes |

> **Viewpoint tip:** The views from the castle ramparts are the best in the entire area. On a clear late March day, you can see all the way to Valencia and beyond. Bring water for the climb.

### 3.3 Jewish Quarter (Juderia)

One of the best-preserved medieval Jewish quarters in the Valencia region.

**History:** Sagunto's Jewish community dates back to at least the Moorish period and continued through the medieval era. The quarter was accessible through the **Portalet de la Juderia**, a small gateway on Calle del Castillo. The community was expelled during the 1492 expulsion ordered by the Catholic Monarchs.

**What you see:** A compact maze of **narrow, winding streets** lined with whitewashed houses, featuring characteristic **ogival arches** spanning the alleyways, small squares, and an intimate medieval atmosphere. The former synagogue site is marked. Hebrew inscriptions from the community are preserved in the Epigraphic Museum in the castle. Look for the traditional house facades with mezuzah marks on doorframes.

**Practical details:**
| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Admission** | Free (open streets) |
| **Access** | Enter through the Portalet de la Juderia on Calle del Castillo |
| **Time needed** | 20-30 minutes of wandering |

### 3.4 Temple of Diana Remains (Restos del Templo de Diana)

A surviving section of an ancient Iberian/Roman temple wall, 15 metres long and 4 metres high, made of large limestone blocks. Dating to the 5th-4th century BC, it is one of the oldest standing structures in the Valencian Community. Legend has it that it survived Hannibal's destruction of the city because it was dedicated to the goddess Diana and the Carthaginians respected its sacred status. Located adjacent to the Church of Santa Maria. Declared a National Monument.

**Practical details:**
| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Admission** | Free (visible from the street) |
| **Time needed** | 5-10 minutes |
| **Location** | Plaza Mayor / next to the Church of Santa Maria |

### 3.5 Church of Santa Maria (Iglesia de Santa Maria)

A Gothic church built between the 14th and 17th centuries on what is believed to be the site of a former mosque (and before that, possibly a Roman temple). Features three entrances: two Gothic doors and a Baroque main entrance with Plateresque columns. The interior has a single nave with side chapels. Adjacent to the Temple of Diana remains.

### 3.6 Church of El Salvador (Iglesia del Salvador)

A 13th-century Gothic church in the El Salvador neighbourhood, notable for its Romanesque portal and polygonal apse. One of the oldest Christian buildings in Sagunto, built shortly after the Reconquest.

### 3.7 Sagunto Historical Museum (Museo Historico de Sagunto)

Located in a 14th-century building (the Casa del Mestre Penya) in the old town, this small museum houses archaeological finds from the area spanning Iberian, Roman, Visigothic, and medieval periods. Collections include pottery, coins, sculptures, tools, and everyday objects.

**Practical details:**
| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Admission** | Free |
| **Opening hours** | Tuesday-Saturday: 10:00-14:00 & 16:00-18:00 (may vary); Sunday: 10:00-14:00 |
| **Closed** | Mondays |
| **Time needed** | 30-45 minutes |
| **Location** | Calle del Castillo, in the old town |

> **Note:** The Epigraphic Museum inside the castle (Plaza de Armas) is a separate small collection of inscriptions and is included in your castle visit.

### 3.8 Old Town (Ciutat Vella / Casco Antiguo)

The old town sits at the foot of the castle hill and is a pleasure to wander. Key features include:

- **Calle del Castillo (Carrer del Castell):** The main artery climbing from the lower town up toward the castle, lined with historic buildings
- **Plaza Mayor:** The main square, surrounded by cafes, with the Temple of Diana remains and Church of Santa Maria
- **Portal de la Sangre:** Medieval gateway
- **Ermita de la Sangre:** Small hermitage with historic significance
- **Various medieval houses and facades** in the winding streets

## 4. Suggested Walking Route (Optimal Order)

This route minimises backtracking and saves the most spectacular views for a rewarding climax.

**Total walking distance:** ~4-5 km | **Total time including visits:** 4-6 hours

### Morning Route

1. **Arrive at Sagunt train station** (~10:00)
   - Walk northwest along Avenida Pais Valencia / Camino Real toward the old town (~10 min)

2. **Plaza Mayor & Temple of Diana** (~10:15)
   - Start at the main square. See the Temple of Diana remains and the exterior of the Church of Santa Maria. Get your bearings

3. **Jewish Quarter (Juderia)** (~10:30)
   - From Plaza Mayor, head up Calle del Castillo to the Portalet de la Juderia and explore the narrow lanes

4. **Continue up Calle del Castillo toward the Roman Theatre** (~11:00)
   - The climb is gradual. Stop at the Historical Museum on the way if open

5. **Roman Theatre (Teatro Romano)** (~11:15)
   - Spend 30-45 minutes. The morning light is good for photos from here

6. **Climb to the Castle (Castillo de Sagunto)** (~12:00)
   - Continue uphill from the theatre. The path is well-marked. The steepest section is about 15 minutes
   - Explore the seven plazas, the Roman Forum remains, the Epigraphic Museum, the Moorish walls
   - Take in the panoramic views from the ramparts

7. **Descend from the castle** (~13:30)
   - Walk back down through the old town

### Afternoon Route

8. **Lunch in the Old Town** (~14:00)
   - Perfect timing for the Spanish lunch hour (14:00-15:30 is when *menu del dia* is served)

9. **Post-lunch wander** (~15:30)
   - Explore any parts of the old town you missed. Visit the Church of El Salvador. Browse the streets at leisure

10. **Return to station** (~16:00-17:00)
    - Walk back to the station for your return train

## 5. Where to Eat -- Restaurant Recommendations

Sagunto's old town has several good restaurants. Expect traditional Valencian and Mediterranean cuisine. The **menu del dia** (daily set menu: starter + main + dessert/drink) is the budget traveller's best friend and is widely available on weekdays.

### Recommended Restaurants in the Old Town

**L'Armeler**
- **Cuisine:** Mediterranean, Valencian, modern tapas
- **Location:** Calle del Castillo area, in the old town
- **Price range:** Menu del dia ~EUR 13-16. A la carte mains EUR 10-18
- **Known for:** Quality local ingredients, good value, pleasant atmosphere
- **TripAdvisor rating:** Highly rated

**Restaurante El Berganti**
- **Cuisine:** Traditional Valencian, rice dishes, seafood
- **Location:** Old town
- **Price range:** Menu del dia ~EUR 12-15
- **Known for:** Local rice dishes (arros a banda, arros negre), regional wines

**La Bohemia**
- **Cuisine:** Mediterranean, tapas, casual dining
- **Location:** Plaza Mayor area
- **Price range:** Tapas EUR 4-8, menu del dia ~EUR 12-14
- **Known for:** Good terrace seating in the square, relaxed atmosphere

**Bar El Forn / local bars in Plaza Mayor**
- **Cuisine:** Tapas, bocadillos (sandwiches), drinks
- **Price range:** Tapas EUR 3-6, beer EUR 2-3, bocadillos EUR 4-6
- **Good for:** A quick, cheap bite or pre-lunch drink

### Budget Tips
- **Menu del dia** is usually served weekdays (Monday-Friday) at lunch only (13:30-16:00). On weekends, availability may be reduced
- In late March (a weekday visit is ideal), you should find menus del dia at EUR 12-16 in most old town restaurants
- Typical menu del dia includes: first course (soup, salad, or pasta) + second course (meat or fish) + dessert or coffee + bread + drink (often wine, water, or soft drink)
- If visiting on a weekend, tapas hopping is the best budget approach
- Traditional local specialties to try: **paella** (especially *arros a banda* -- rice in fish broth), **arros negre** (black rice with squid ink), **esgarraet** (roasted red pepper and salt cod salad), **horchata** (tiger nut milk, though this is more a Valencia city thing)

## 6. Nearby Combinations -- Extending Your Trip

### Puerto de Sagunto (Port of Sagunto) -- 5 km east

The coastal industrial town of Puerto de Sagunto has its own character and a few draws:
- **Beach:** A long, wide sandy beach (Playa de Puerto de Sagunto) -- good for a late March walk along the seafront, though likely too cold for swimming
- **Blast Furnace Museum / Industrial Heritage:** The town has interesting early 20th-century industrial heritage from its steel-making past (Altos Hornos del Mediterraneo). The old blast furnaces and industrial zone have been partly preserved
- **Getting there:** Bus connects Sagunto old town to Puerto de Sagunto in ~10 minutes. You could also walk (45-60 min) but it is flat and not scenic
- **Worth combining?** Only if you are particularly interested in industrial heritage or want a beach walk. Otherwise, the old town alone fills a day trip well

### Canet d'en Berenguer -- 8 km northeast

A small beach town just north of Puerto de Sagunto:
- Quieter, cleaner beaches than Puerto de Sagunto
- Promenade walk
- Some seafood restaurants
- Accessible by bus from Sagunto or Puerto de Sagunto
- **Worth combining?** More of a summer destination. In late March, it will be very quiet. Only worth a detour if you want a peaceful seaside walk

### Almenara -- 10 km north

A smaller inland town with:
- Castle ruins (Castillo de Almenara) on a hilltop
- The Almenara marshlands (Marjal d'Almenara) -- a wetland area with birdwatching potential
- **Worth combining?** Only for dedicated birdwatchers or castle enthusiasts. Sagunto itself provides more than enough for a day

### Sierra Calderona Natural Park -- west of Sagunto

The mountain range visible to the west of Sagunto is the **Sierra Calderona**, a natural park with hiking trails:
- The Cartuja de Portaceli (Carthusian monastery) is a notable sight
- Various hiking routes through Mediterranean forest
- Requires a car to access trailheads
- **Worth combining?** Not easily combined with Sagunto on the same day without a car. Better as a separate trip

### Recommendation

For a late March day trip, **Sagunto old town alone provides more than enough** to fill 5-7 rewarding hours. If you finish early and want more, a bus ride down to Puerto de Sagunto for a beach-front walk and a drink at a chiringuito (beach bar) is a pleasant add-on.

## 10. Verdict -- Is Sagunto Worth It?

**Absolutely yes.** For a couple who loves history, culture, and nature, Sagunto is arguably the **best-value day trip from Valencia**. Here is why:

- **Depth of history:** Few places this size offer visible layers spanning Iberian, Roman, Moorish, Jewish, and medieval Christian civilizations
- **The Hannibal connection:** Standing on the hilltop where one of the most dramatic sieges in ancient history took place -- the event that triggered the Second Punic War -- is genuinely thrilling for history buffs
- **Cost:** Effectively free once you arrive (all attractions are free admission). Total day trip cost of EUR 25-30 per person including transport and a good lunch
- **Manageable size:** Everything is walkable and the town is compact enough to see thoroughly without exhaustion
- **Uncrowded:** Unlike some day trip destinations, Sagunto is not overrun with tourists, even on weekends. You will likely have the castle largely to yourselves on a March weekday
- **The views:** The panoramic views from the castle ramparts -- sea, mountains, orange groves, the old town below -- are spectacular

**Compared to Xativa:** Both are excellent. Xativa has a more impressive single castle and the Borgia connection. Sagunto has the Roman Theatre (unique in the region), a better-preserved Jewish quarter, and is closer/quicker to reach. You could easily do both on separate days.

## What is Albufera?

Albufera Natural Park (Parc Natural de l'Albufera) is Spain's largest freshwater lagoon, located just 10-15 km south of Valencia city center. Spanning over 21,000 hectares, it is one of the most important wetlands in the entire Mediterranean basin, designated as a Ramsar site and an EU Special Protection Area for birds.

The name "Albufera" comes from the Arabic "al-buhayra" (the small sea). In Roman times it was a saltwater bay open to the Mediterranean; over centuries, sediment from the Turia River gradually sealed it off, forming the freshwater lagoon we see today. The thin sandy strip separating the lake from the sea is called **La Devesa** -- a unique ecosystem of dunes, pine forests, and Mediterranean scrubland.

### Why It's Special

- **The birthplace of paella.** The rice paddies surrounding the lagoon have been cultivated since the 8th century (Moorish era). Local farmers combined rice with local ingredients -- rabbit, snails, green beans, saffron -- to create Valencian paella. El Palmar village is ground zero for this tradition.
- **Over 300 bird species** use the park as a breeding ground, wintering site, or stopover on migratory routes. It sits on a major migration flyway between Europe and Africa.
- **A living agricultural landscape.** The annual rice-growing cycle (flooding, planting, harvesting, draining) shapes the entire ecosystem and determines which birds are present at any given time.
- **Traditional fishing culture.** The Fishermen's Community (Comunitat de Pescadors) was founded in the 13th century and remains active. Traditional flat-bottomed boats called "albuferencs" still navigate the channels.

### The Ecosystem in Late March

In late March, the rice paddies are being ploughed and flooded in preparation for planting. This creates ideal feeding conditions for wading birds. The lagoon itself, the surrounding marshes (tancats), the dune system of La Devesa, and the rice fields each form distinct micro-habitats teeming with life.

### 2. Birdwatching

Late March is excellent for birding here. The rice fields are being ploughed, attracting masses of wading birds and ibises.

**What to see in March:**
- **Waders:** Black-winged Stilts, Avocets, Black-tailed Godwits (in breeding plumage), Collared Pratincoles
- **Herons & egrets:** Grey Heron, Purple Heron, Cattle Egret, Little Egret, Great Egret
- **Other waterbirds:** Greater Flamingo, Glossy Ibis (abundant in ploughed fields), Spoonbill, Marbled Teal, White-headed Duck, Red-knobbed Coot
- **Raptors:** Marsh Harrier, Peregrine Falcon
- **Spring migrants arriving:** Whiskered Tern, Little Tern, Great Reed Warbler, Moustached Warbler
- On a good migration day, up to 100 species can be observed in a single day

**Best birdwatching spots:**
- **Raco de l'Olla Interpretation Centre** -- free entry, observation hides, educational exhibits (see below)

- **Gola de Pujol viewpoint** -- sunset spot overlooking the lagoon

A tiny fishing village of a few hundred inhabitants, completely surrounded by rice paddies and lagoon channels. This is the cultural and gastronomic heart of Albufera.

A small coastal settlement between Valencia and El Palmar, at the northern end of La Devesa.

### 6. La Devesa (Dehesa del Saler)

# Day Trip: Sierra Calderona Natural Park from Valencia

> **Best for:** Nature lovers, hikers, couples wanting a mountain escape from the city
> **Duration:** Full day recommended (6-8 hours in the park)
> **Distance from Valencia:** ~25 km northwest (~45 min by metro + bus)
> **Best time:** Late March is ideal -- wildflowers blooming, pleasant 15-20C temperatures, no summer heat
> **Budget for two:** ~EUR 20-35 (transport + packed lunch or restaurant meal)

---

## 1. What is Sierra Calderona? -- Description & Significance

Sierra Calderona (Valencian: Serra Calderona) is a mountain range and **protected natural park** stretching between the provinces of Valencia and Castellon, running roughly parallel to the coast about 20-30 km inland. It was declared a Natural Park (*Parc Natural*) in 2002, covering approximately **18,019 hectares**.

### Landscape & Geography

The range forms a natural barrier between the coastal plain of Valencia and the interior plateaus. Key features include:

- **Highest peak:** El Gorgo (or Garbino) at 907 m, though most hikes stay in the 400-600 m range
- **Garbi peak:** 601 m -- the most popular and iconic viewpoint on the Valencia-facing side, offering sweeping views of the Mediterranean, the city of Valencia, the Albufera lagoon, and the Turia valley
- **Terrain:** A mix of limestone ridges, sandstone outcrops (Rodeno -- distinctive red rock), pine-forested slopes, deep ravines (barrancos), and Mediterranean maquis scrubland
- **Water features:** Numerous natural springs (*fonts*) throughout the park -- Font de Deula, Font de Marianet, Font del Llentiscle, Font de Poll, Font de Potrillos -- many used since Moorish times
- **Historic ice pits (ventisqueros):** 18th-century stone structures once used to store snow and ice for transport to Valencia; some with capacities over 1,100 cubic metres

### Flora

The park supports rich Mediterranean vegetation:

- **Aleppo pine** (*Pinus halepensis*) -- dominates lower slopes
- **Maritime pine** (*Pinus pinaster*) -- on sandstone areas
- **Cork oak** (*Quercus suber*) -- one of the southernmost populations in Europe, found on the red Rodeno sandstone
- **Holm oak** (*Quercus ilex*) -- in shaded valleys (ombries)
- **Maquis shrubland:** rosemary, thyme, lavender, cistus, gorse, mastic tree
- **In late March:** Expect wildflowers -- orchids, rock roses, lavender beginning to bloom; the landscape is green from winter rains

### Fauna

- **Bonelli's eagle** (*Aquila fasciata*) -- endangered; Sierra Calderona is one of its key breeding areas in Valencia
- **Red squirrel** (*Sciurus vulgaris*)
- **Wild boar** (*Sus scrofa*) -- common but rarely seen during day hikes
- **Genet** (*Genetta genetta*) -- nocturnal
- **Peregrine falcon, eagle owl, kestrel** -- various raptors
- **Iberian wall lizard, ocellated lizard** -- on sunny rock faces
- **Hermann's tortoise** -- rare sightings

### History

The mountains have been inhabited for millennia:

- **Iberian settlements** on hilltops (pre-Roman)
- **Roman road** fragments traversing the range
- **Moorish period (8th-13th century):** The most significant cultural layer. The Moors built a network of **castles and watchtowers** across the ridgeline, including the Castle of Serra (8th-9th century), controlling access to Valencia's hinterland. They also developed the irrigation system of springs and channels
- **El Cid** conquered the castle of Serra around 1095 during his campaigns
- **Christian reconquest (1238):** James I of Aragon took the area; Moorish population remained under feudal rule
- **Cartuja de Portaceli (1272):** Carthusian monastery founded in the park -- the first in the Valencia region. It produced the first Valencian translation of the Bible and remained active for centuries
- **18th century:** Construction of ice pits (ventisqueros) for the ice trade
- **2002:** Declared a Natural Park by the Valencian Government

## 2. Hiking Trails

Sierra Calderona offers trails for all levels. The municipality of Serra maintains a well-documented set of routes. Below are the best options for a moderate day trip (10,000-15,000 steps / 7-15 km).

### Trail 1: Castell de Serra (Castle Route) -- RECOMMENDED

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | 7 km (circular) |
| **Elevation gain** | 207 m |
| **Duration** | ~2 hours |
| **Difficulty** | Moderate (family-friendly) |
| **Start/End** | Serra Town Hall (Plaça del Poble) |
| **Markings** | Follows GR-10 markers (red/white) for part of the route |

**Description:** Starting from Serra's Town Hall, the trail passes through traditional orchards and climbs toward the medieval castle ruins (536 m). You pass by the Torre de Ria (one of four Moorish defensive towers). There are two options for the ascent: a traditional mountain path or a wider forest road via the Ombria spring. At the top, the castle (8th-9th century, ~600 sqm) features a powerful main tower (~10 m high, square base), crenellated walls, and a cistern. The panoramic views over the Turia valley are outstanding. Return via a forest track through Pont Nou back to town.

**Why this trail:** Perfect moderate hike with cultural interest. The castle is genuinely atmospheric, and the views are worth every step. Short enough to combine with a village lunch or a second short walk.

**Wikiloc / GPS:** Search "Castell de Serra circular" on Wikiloc or download the PDF from serratotnatura.com

### Trail 2: Garbi Viewpoint (Subida al Garbi desde Serra)

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | ~10-12 km (circular, depending on route) |
| **Elevation gain** | ~400-450 m |
| **Duration** | ~3-4 hours |
| **Difficulty** | Moderate to demanding |
| **Start/End** | Serra town or Area Recreativa del Garbi |
| **Summit** | 601 m |

**Description:** The Garbi is the signature hike of the southern Sierra Calderona. The trail ascends through pine forest and Mediterranean scrub to the Garbi summit (601 m), where a viewpoint offers a spectacular 360-degree panorama: the Mediterranean Sea, Valencia city skyline, the Albufera lagoon, the Turia valley, and the interior mountain ranges stretching to the horizon. On a clear late March day, visibility can extend to the Columbretes Islands.

The most common route starts from Serra village, follows forest tracks and the GR-10 path, and ascends via switchbacks through increasingly open terrain to the summit. A shorter option starts from the Area Recreativa del Garbi (a picnic area accessible by car on the CV-310), cutting the distance roughly in half but missing some of the forest walk.

**Why this trail:** The best views in the entire park. This is THE hike to do if you want a proper mountain experience near Valencia. Late March temperatures make it comfortable -- in summer this trail is brutally hot.

**Wikiloc / GPS:** Search "Subida al Garbi desde Serra" on Wikiloc -- many recorded routes available

## 4. Towns to Visit

### Serra (Main Base -- Recommended)

Serra (pop. ~3,200) is the main gateway to the southern Sierra Calderona and the best base for hiking. It sits at the foot of the mountains, surrounded by traditional orchards and pine forests.

**What to see:**
- **Castle of Serra (Castell de Serra):** 8th-9th century Moorish fortress at 536 m elevation, with a powerful square tower (~10 m high), crenellated walls, cistern. Part of an extensive defensive network. Conquered by El Cid c.1095
- **Four defensive towers:** Torre del Senyor, Torre de l'Ermita, Torre de Ria, Torre de Satarenya -- remnants of the Moorish defensive system scattered around the municipality
- **Calvary and 19th-century hermitage:** Hilltop chapel with views
- **Parish church**
- **Ruta de les Escultures (Sculpture Route):** A self-guided walking itinerary through the town exploring local heritage via urban sculptures. Recently improved with Braille signage and audio guides
- **Museu de l'Oli (Oil Museum):** Small museum dedicated to olive oil production
- **Natural springs (Fonts):** Font de Deula, Font de l'Ombria, Font de Marianet -- pleasant spots for resting

**Atmosphere:** A quiet, authentic Valencian mountain village. Not touristy at all. Narrow streets, white houses, orange trees. You will feel like you have discovered somewhere special.

### Naquera

Naquera (pop. ~6,500) is another municipality on the edge of the park, east of Serra, slightly closer to Valencia. It is connected by Bus Line 230 from Betera.

**What to see:**
- **Pi del Salt:** The famous "Salt Pine" -- an ancient pine tree on a cliff edge, a popular hiking destination
- **Font del Garrofer:** Natural spring and recreational area
- **Old town:** Small but pleasant centre with a parish church
- Starting point for several park trails on the eastern side

### Betera

Betera (pop. ~24,000) is the largest town in the area, located at the metro terminus. It is not in the mountains itself but serves as the transport hub.

**What to see (if you have time waiting for a connection):**
- **Torre de Bofilla:** A notable Moorish watchtower (13th century), one of the best preserved in the Valencia region
- **Parish church and old quarter**
- **Local market** (check days)

**Practical note:** Betera has cafes, supermarkets, and ATMs near the metro station -- good for picking up supplies before heading to Serra.

## 8. What to Bring

### Essential Gear

- **Hiking shoes or sturdy trainers:** Trails are mostly forest tracks and paths, some rocky sections. Full hiking boots not required for the moderate trails, but ankle support helps on Garbi and Rebalsadors
- **Sun protection:** Hat, sunscreen (SPF 30+), sunglasses. Even in late March, the Mediterranean sun is strong at altitude
- **Water:** Minimum 1.5 litres per person. There are natural springs along some trails, but do not rely on them -- some may be dry or diverted
- **Snacks:** Energy bars, nuts, fruit. There are no shops or kiosks on the trails
- **Light jacket or fleece:** Mountain weather can change. Late March mornings can be cool (~10-12C at altitude), warming to 18-20C by midday. Bring a windbreaker
- **Rain jacket (packable):** March occasionally brings showers -- a light rain shell weighs nothing and saves the day

### Recommended Extras

- **Packed lunch / picnic:** Bocadillos, cheese, fruit, pastries from a Valencia bakery. There are picnic tables at recreational areas (Area Recreativa del Garbi, Aliguetes, etc.)
- **Offline maps:** Download the area on Google Maps or Maps.me before going. Mobile signal is spotty in the mountains
- **Trail GPS:** Download Wikiloc app and the specific trail you plan to follow (search for route names listed above)
- **Phone battery pack:** GPS navigation drains battery fast
- **Small first aid kit:** Plasters, antiseptic -- standard hiking precaution
- **Cash:** Some village bars and restaurants may not accept cards. Bring EUR 30-50 in cash per person to be safe
- **Binoculars (optional):** For birdwatching -- Bonelli's eagles and other raptors nest here

### Food & Drink Strategy (Budget)

- **Breakfast:** Eat before leaving Valencia (cafe con leche + tostada, ~EUR 3)
- **Trail snacks:** Buy at a supermarket in Valencia the evening before (Mercadona, Consum -- a baguette, jamon, cheese, fruit, and water for two will cost ~EUR 5-8)
- **Lunch option A (budget):** Picnic at a recreational area -- free!
- **Lunch option B (treat):** Menu del dia at a Serra restaurant -- EUR 10-14 per person
- **Water refill:** Top up at springs (Font de Deula in Serra is reliable)

## 9. Practical Tips

### Late March Conditions

- **Weather:** Daytime highs 17-22C, morning lows 8-12C. Generally sunny with occasional spring showers. Check AEMET forecast (aemet.es) before going
- **Daylight:** Sunrise ~07:45, sunset ~20:00 (Spain uses CET, clocks go forward to CEST last Sunday of March). Plenty of daylight for a full day
- **Wildflowers:** Late March is the start of spring bloom. Expect cistus, rosemary, lavender, orchids, and green hillsides
- **Crowds:** Minimal on weekdays. Some local hikers on weekends, but nothing like summer. You will likely have trails mostly to yourself

### Safety

- **Fire risk:** Even in March, do not light fires except in designated barbecue areas. The park has suffered devastating forest fires in the past
- **Emergency number:** 112 (European emergency number, works everywhere)
- **Tell someone your route:** Inform your accommodation of your hiking plans
- **Stay on marked trails:** The park has well-signposted routes; going off-trail risks erosion and getting lost
- **Wild boar:** Present but shy. They avoid hikers. If you see one, give it space

> **Best for:** History lovers, culture buffs, nature walkers, budget travellers
> **Duration:** Full day recommended (6-8 hours)
> **Distance from Valencia:** ~60 km southwest (~1 hour by train)
> **Best time:** Late March is ideal -- pleasant weather, no summer heat (Xativa records the highest temperatures in the Valencia region in summer)

## 1. Why Xativa? -- History & Significance

Xativa (Valencian: Xativa, Castilian: Jativa) is one of the most historically rich towns in the entire Valencian Community. Its layers of history span over two millennia:

**Roman Era (1st century BC):** Known as *Saetabis*, it was an important stop on the Via Augusta connecting Rome to Hispania. The Romans prized it for its linen production.

**Moorish Golden Age (8th-13th century):** Under Moorish rule as *Shatiba*, the city flourished as a centre of learning, education, and culture. Most famously, **Xativa became home to the first paper mill in Europe in the 11th century**, introducing paper manufacturing to the continent. The technique was brought from the Islamic world, and even today in Morocco, paper is still called *xativi*. The Islamic scholar Abu Ishaq al-Shatibi took his name from this city.

**The Borgia (Borja) Dynasty:** Xativa is the **birthplace of the Borja family** (Italianised as Borgia), one of the most powerful and notorious families in Renaissance Europe. The family produced **two popes**: Callixtus III (Alfonso de Borja, 1455-1458) and the infamous Alexander VI (Rodrigo de Borja, 1492-1503), who was born in Xativa in 1431. Alexander VI is known for his political machinations, nepotism, and his children Cesare and Lucrezia Borgia.

**The Painter:** Xativa is also the birthplace of **Jose de Ribera** ("El Espanoleto"), one of Spain's greatest Baroque painters.

**The Burning of 1707:** During the War of Spanish Succession, Xativa sided against the Bourbon Philip V. After his victory, Philip ordered the city to be **burned to the ground** and renamed it "San Felipe" as punishment. The city's revenge? They hung his portrait **upside down** in the museum -- where it remains to this day.

## 3. Attractions -- Complete Guide

### 3.1 Castell de Xativa (Xativa Castle) -- THE must-see

The spectacular double castle stretching along a mountain ridge above the city is Xativa's crown jewel and one of the finest castles in the entire Valencia region.

**Structure:** The castle complex consists of two fortifications connected by a wall along the narrow mountain saddle:
- **Castell Menor (Lesser Castle):** The oldest section, with Iberian and Roman foundations. Includes ancient cisterns and towers.
- **Castell Major (Greater Castle):** The larger, better-preserved section. Features Gothic gates, towers, chapels, prison cells (the Count of Urgell was imprisoned here), and stunning panoramic views over the entire Costera valley and beyond.

**What to expect:** You enter at the lowest point of the saddle and can explore in both directions. Allow **at least 2 hours** to see everything. The views from the towers are breathtaking on a clear March day.

| **Opening hours (Mar)** | Tue-Sun: 10:00 - 19:00 (closed Mondays) |

**Getting to the castle:**
- **On foot:** A steep uphill walk of ~25-30 minutes from the old town. The path follows historic stone pathways -- moderate difficulty, wear comfortable shoes. The climb is the most demanding part of the day trip, but very doable for reasonably fit visitors.
- **Shuttle bus:** Runs from Plaza Espanyoleto to the castle (EUR 1 each way). Available on Sundays/holidays for sure; check locally for weekday availability.
- **Tourist train:** Departs from the tourist office twice daily (winter: 12:30 and 16:30; summer: 12:30 and 17:30). EUR 4.20 return. Winds through town and up to the castle.

### 3.2 Ermita de Sant Feliu (San Felix Church) -- pre-Romanesque gem

Located at the foot of the castle hill, this is the **oldest church in the Kingdom of Valencia**, built around 1265 on the site of a Roman temple, then Visigothic church, then mosque.

**Highlights:**
- **Romanesque doorway** -- one of the very few examples of this architectural style in all of Valencia
- **Side atrium** with six mismatched marble columns recycled from ancient Roman buildings, some with the distinctive pink colour from local Buixcarro quarries
- **Medieval murals** dating to the first half of the 14th century -- the **largest assemblage of medieval linear Gothic painting** still preserved in the Valencian region. They were hidden under whitewash for centuries and are now partially restored.

## 4. Recommended Walking Route -- Optimal Order

### Morning: Castle First (do this while you have energy!)

1. **09:30** - Arrive at Xativa train station. Walk ~10 min to the old town
2. **09:45** - Quick coffee at Placa del Mercat or along the main streets
3. **10:00** - Head up to **Xativa Castle** (opens at 10:00). Walk up via the road that passes Sant Feliu, or take the shuttle bus from Plaza Espanyoleto
4. **10:10** - Stop at **Ermita de Sant Feliu** on the way up (it's right on the path). Spend 15-20 min
5. **10:30-12:30** - Explore the **castle** thoroughly -- both Castell Menor and Castell Major. Take your time with the views
6. **12:30** - Walk back down to the old town (easier downhill, ~20 min)

### Midday: Lunch

7. **12:45-14:00** - **Lunch** in the old town (see restaurant recommendations below). Enjoy a Menu del Dia.

### Afternoon: Old Town & Museums

8. **14:15** - **Colegiata Basilica de Santa Maria** (La Seu)
9. **14:45** - Stroll through the **old town**: Carrer Montcada, Placa de la Trinitat (Gothic fountain), Borgia Palace exterior, Royal Hospital facade
10. **15:30** - **Museo de Bellas Artes** -- see the upside-down Philip V portrait and other works
11. **16:15** - **Museo Arqueologico de l'Almodi** -- the Islamic basin and Roman finds
12. **16:45** - Walk the **Fountains Route** -- Royal Fountain (25 jets), other fountains around town
13. **17:15** - Final stroll, souvenir shopping, or a drink at a terrace
14. **17:30-18:00** - Walk back to the train station, catch a train back to Valencia

### About the Castle Climb

- **Steepness:** Moderate to steep. The road winds uphill with some steep sections. Total elevation gain is roughly 150-200 metres.
- **Duration:** 25-30 minutes walking up from the old town; 15-20 minutes back down
- **Surface:** Paved road and historic stone pathways
- **Difficulty:** Manageable for anyone with reasonable fitness. Not suitable for those with severe mobility issues. Wear comfortable, closed shoes.
- **Tip:** Do the castle FIRST in the morning when you're fresh and it's cooler. March temperatures should be pleasant (15-20C).

### Bocairent (~35 km south of Xativa) -- Medieval Village & Caves
- **Covetes dels Moros (Moorish Caves):** ~50 artificial caves with window-like openings carved into a vertical cliff face. Truly unique.
  - Guided tours: 1 hour, max 15 people
  - Hours: Tue-Fri 11:00-14:00; Sat-Sun 11:00-14:00 & 16:00-18:00

- **Getting there:** By car only (~30 min from Xativa). Note: the Xativa-Alcoi train line is suspended until late 2026/2027 for renovation.

### Ontinyent (~25 km southwest of Xativa)
- Historic textile town with a medieval old quarter
- Iglesia de Santa Maria and medieval bridges
- Less touristy, more authentic feel
- By car only practical option

### Recommendation for Your Trip
**Xativa alone fills a satisfying full day.** If you want to combine, the best pairing is **Xativa + Anna** (only feasible with a car or organised tour). Save Bocairent for a separate trip -- it deserves its own half-day.

## REQUENA - Medieval Wine Town

**Distance:** ~70 km west of Valencia | **Time needed:** Full day (7-8 hours in town) | **Best for:** History, wine culture, medieval architecture, unique underground caves

### Why Requena?

Requena is one of the most rewarding day trips from Valencia. Perched at 700m altitude on the edge of the Castilian meseta, this fortified medieval town has a history stretching back to the Bronze Age, with Iberian, Roman, Moorish, and Christian layers all visible. Its crown jewel is **La Villa**, a remarkably preserved medieval quarter encircled by walls, sitting on a honeycomb of underground caves once used for wine storage. The town sits at the heart of the **Utiel-Requena D.O.** (Denomination of Origin) wine region, one of Spain's oldest, with a winemaking tradition of 2,500+ years. The native **Bobal** grape dominates, producing bold reds and excellent roses.

Unlike polished tourist towns, Requena feels genuinely lived-in: quiet cobbled streets, centuries-old churches, locals going about their day. In late March the weather will be cool (expect 8-15C, significantly colder than Valencia - bring layers!), the landscape green from spring rains, and you'll have the town largely to yourselves.

### All Attractions

#### Cuevas de la Villa (Underground Caves) - MUST SEE

A labyrinth of 22 interconnected underground chambers beneath La Villa quarter, carved from limestone and tufa clay over centuries. Used since Moorish times (7th-11th century) for grain storage, water collection, bone storage (ossuary), and wine making. The caves maintain a constant 15-16C, making them comfortable even in cold weather. The 1.2 km guided route passes through seven distinct areas: an ossuary (used until the early 1800s), a communal granary (under the old Town Hall, 1685-19th century), grain silos with amphora-shaped sealed chambers, a well, and wine cellars with a jaraiz (grape-stomping pool) and clay fermentation jars. Absolutely unique - nothing like this in Valencia.

- **Price:** 4 EUR (reduced 3 EUR for pensioners, youth card, large families, disabled 33%+, unemployed, groups 15+)
- **Combined tickets (Bonos):** Caves + 1 museum = 5 EUR | + 2 museums = 6 EUR | + 3 museums = 7 EUR | + 4 museums = 8 EUR. Unlimited validity!
- **Hours:** Wed-Sun 10:30-14:00 & 16:00-19:00. Closed Mondays & Tuesdays. No entry within 30 min of closing.
- **Closed:** Jan 1 & 6, Mar 19, Dec 24/25/31, all non-holiday Mondays
- **Address:** Plaza de Albornoz 6, La Villa
- **Note:** Not wheelchair accessible. Steep stairs involved.
- **Contact:** +34 96 230 60 18 | cuevasdelavilla@requena.es
- https://www.requena.es/cuevas-villa

> **Budget tip:** Get the Caves + 4 museums combined ticket for 8 EUR/person. That covers the Cuevas + Wine Museum + Torre del Homenaje + Iglesia Santa Maria + Municipal Museum (or other combo). Incredible value!

#### La Villa (Medieval Quarter) - FREE

The walled historic heart of Requena, entered through medieval gates. A compact labyrinth of narrow cobbled streets, stone mansions, hidden plazas, and centuries of layered history. Contains archaeological remains from the Iron Age, a Jewish Quarter (Juderia), the Moorish Alcazaba ruins, and the Medina area. The entire quarter is a designated Historic-Artistic Conservation Area. Walking its quiet alleys, especially on a weekday, feels like stepping back 500 years.

- **Key spots within La Villa:** Plaza de la Villa (Plaza de Albornoz) - the central square; Cuesta de las Carniceras - the atmospheric stepped lane; the Jewish Quarter along Calle de la Villa; remains of the Alcazaba (Moorish fortress); medieval gates and walls.
- **Price:** FREE to walk around

#### Iglesia del Salvador (Church of the Savior)

An outstanding example of Isabelline/Flamboyant Gothic architecture. Construction began 1380 on an earlier chapel and was completed in 1533. The main facade is the highlight: three flared archivolts decorated with floral chains of leaves, bows, and bunches of grapes. The outer niches hold six pairs of sculpted virgins; the inner section has five pairs of angels with symbols of the Passion. The tympanum depicts the Transfiguration of the Lord, with Christ surrounded by a mandorla of angels. Inside: a central nave with side chapels, Baroque ornamentation by master Juan Perez Castiel, and a stunning Neoclassical Communion Chapel (mid-18th century) with an octagonal interior, Corinthian pilasters, and a blue-tiled dome. The bell tower (late 18th century) has six bells and a blue tile hipped roof.

- **Price:** The church is still active for worship. Check locally for visiting hours (often included in combined ticket).
- **Address:** La Villa quarter, adjacent to the Cuevas de la Villa entrance

#### Iglesia de Santa Maria

The second great Gothic church of La Villa, begun in the late 15th century in Isabelline Gothic style with work continuing into the 18th century (Baroque nave). The main Gothic portal is richly sculpted with angels, floral motifs, and a scene of the Annunciation in the tympanum. Declared a National Monument in 1931. The church lost much interior decoration during the Civil War. Since 2004, after restoration, it hosts exhibitions and concerts (notably the Week of Sacred Music). The deteriorated but still beautiful Gothic facade alone is worth seeing.

- **Price:** Included in the combined ticket system (part of the Bono options with Cuevas de la Villa)
- **Hours:** Check at tourist office - functions as exhibition space, hours may vary

#### Torre del Homenaje (Keep Tower)

The medieval watchtower and former prison of Requena's fortress. Now converted into an information center with displays on the town's history across different periods. Offers elevated views of the surrounding countryside and the town's rooftops.

- **Price:** 2 EUR (or included in combined ticket)
- **Hours:** Similar to other municipal sites (check at tourist office)

#### Museo del Vino (Wine Museum) - Palacio del Cid

Housed in the 15th-century Gothic Palacio del Cid (Palace of the Cid) on Calle Somera. The museum traces the entire history of winemaking in the region through archaeological remains, industrial tools, documents, and exhibits covering every stage from vine to bottle. A good complement to the Cuevas de la Villa to understand why wine is so central to this town.

- **Price:** 2 EUR (or included in combined ticket)
- **Hours:** Tue-Sun from 11:00 (confirm closing time locally)
- **Address:** Plaza Consistorial 2, Requena
- **Contact:** +34 96 230 32 81

#### Casa del Arte Mayor de la Seda (House of Silk Art)

Yes, Requena has a Silk Museum! Housed in an authentic Moorish blind alley (adarve) in La Villa, this building became the headquarters of the Guild of Silk Weavers in 1740. Three floors trace Requena's silk heritage from the 17th to 19th centuries:

### Where to Eat

# Day Trip to Gandia from Valencia

**Best for:** History, culture, nature, beach, gastronomy
**Distance:** 65 km south of Valencia (~1h15 by train)
**Recommended time:** Full day (leave early, return by evening)
**Budget for two:** ~EUR 50-70 (transport + attractions + lunch)

---

## 1. About Gandia

Gandia (pop. ~75,000) is the historic capital of the La Safor comarca on the Mediterranean coast south of Valencia. The town has two distinct parts: a medieval **old town** 3 km inland, and a lively **beach district** (Playa de Gandia / Grau) on the coast, connected by a local bus.

### The Borgia Connection

Gandia's identity is inseparable from the **Borgia family** (Borja in Valencian/Spanish). The Borgias originated from nearby Xativa but became Dukes of Gandia in the late 14th century. Two family members became popes -- **Callixtus III** (Alfonso de Borja, 1455) and **Alexander VI** (Rodrigo de Borja, 1492) -- making them one of the most powerful families in Renaissance Europe. Alexander VI's son, the infamous **Cesare Borgia**, inspired Machiavelli's "The Prince."

The family's greatest contrast came with **Saint Francis Borgia** (1510-1572), the 4th Duke of Gandia, who renounced his title, joined the Jesuits, and eventually became the third Superior General of the Society of Jesus. He founded Gandia's university in 1547 -- the first Jesuit university in the world. The **Ducal Palace** remains the crown jewel of the Borgia legacy in Gandia.

### Late March Weather

Expect daytime highs around 17-20C, lows around 10C. Generally pleasant for walking. Some chance of rain, so bring a light jacket. The beach will be quiet (not swimming season yet) but perfect for a stroll.

## MONTANEJOS HOT SPRINGS

**Distance:** ~90 km northwest of Valencia (Castellon province) | **Time needed:** Full day | **Best for:** Nature, thermal swimming, hiking, mountain village atmosphere

### Why Montanejos?

Montanejos is a serene mountain village whose star attraction is **Fuente de los Banos** -- a natural thermal spring pool on the Mijares River, set within a limestone gorge with walls rising ~15 meters. The bathing area exceeds 100 meters in length, with some spots reaching 3 meters deep. This is a place to hike dramatic gorges in the morning, then soak in warm mineral waters surrounded by cliffs in the afternoon -- a perfect nature-focused day for a couple.

The thermal water flows at a constant **25C (77F) year-round** from a natural cave behind the fountain, at a rate of approximately 6,000 litres per minute through seven spouts. The constant temperature comes from water being heated deep underground by geothermal processes before emerging at the surface. Because the thermal flow is so strong, it does not mix significantly with the cold Mijares river water, creating a unique thermal riverbed stretching approximately one kilometre.

**The Legend of Abu Zeid:** According to local legend, the Almohad governor Abu Zayd (the last Almohad governor of Valencia) constructed Arab baths here in the 13th century. He believed the waters granted eternal youth and beauty to the women of his harem. While this legend lacks written documentation, the first verified document mentioning the healing properties dates to **1714** -- "Instructions for taking the waters from the medicinal springs of Montanejos" by Mossen Pedro Gil y Vergara. In 1795, naturalist Antonio Jose Cavanilles described the waters as "moderately warm, extremely crystalline, pleasant to the palate." Spain declared them of public utility by Royal Order in 1863.

**Mineral content:** The waters are rich in bicarbonate (aids digestion), magnesium sulfate/Epsom salt (anti-inflammatory, relaxing), calcium, potassium, and iron (nourishes skin, improves elasticity). Historically used to treat eczema, psoriasis, and dermatitis.

**Blue Flag:** Fuente de los Banos is the first inland beach in Castellon province and Spain's only thermal riverbed beach with Blue Flag certification.

**In late March**, you will likely have the springs largely to yourselves -- no crowds, no entry fee (fees of 4.50 EUR only apply during Easter week and summer, 10:00-18:00). However, the changing cabins and cafe may not be open outside high season. The 25C water on a March day is perfectly swimmable -- expect steam rising from the surface, which is magical. Bring warm clothes for after your swim since the air will be cooler than the water.

### All Activities

#### Swimming in the Hot Springs (Fuente de los Banos)

- Soak in the thermal pools at 25C year-round
- Some areas are shallow wading, others up to 3 meters deep
- Behind the fountain there is a natural cave housing the main spring -- you can swim into it
- Hidden cove with a small waterfall within the bathing area
- Cliff-jumping is possible at some points (though officially discouraged)
- Stone skimming on the river
- The area is free to access in March (fees only apply Easter week and summer)

#### Hiking: Ruta de los Estrechos (The Narrows Trail) -- HIGHLIGHT

- **Length:** ~8 km circular loop
- **Duration:** 2.5-3 hours
- **Difficulty:** Moderate (medium-low), suitable for most fitness levels
- **Elevation gain:** ~400 meters
- **Description:** Follows the dramatic canyon carved by the Mijares River through the Chillapajaros gorge. Features vertical cliff walls over 100 meters high, a narrow gorge only 25 meters wide, stunning panoramic views, dense pine forests, and the "horizontal waterfall" from the Arenoso reservoir spillway
- **Starting point:** From Montanejos village, follows the Mijares riverbed up to the peaks bordering Los Estrechos de Chillapajaros
- More info: https://www.visitmontanejos.com/en/nature/los-estrechos/
- AllTrails: https://www.alltrails.com/trail/spain/castellon/sendero-los-estrechos-de-montanejos

#### Cueva Negra (Black Cave)

- Natural cave with Neolithic history, accessible on foot from the village
- Bring a flashlight -- there is no lighting inside

#### Montanejos Old Town

- Quiet cobblestone streets, small mountain village atmosphere
- 17th-century **Iglesia de Santiago Apostol** church
- Central plaza with cafes
- A pleasant 20-30 minute stroll

#### Arenoso Reservoir & El Chorro (Pantano de Arenoso)

- 10-15 minutes away by car
- Swimming, boating, kayaking opportunities
- **El Chorro** -- a massive waterspout where water cascades from the reservoir's spillway into the gorge below. Dramatic sight, especially after rains.

#### Salto de la Novia (Bridal Veil Waterfall) -- Navajas

- Located in nearby **Navajas** village (on the route between Valencia and Montanejos, ~30 min before Montanejos)
- Natural pools at the waterfall base, natural water slide
- Can be a stop on the way there or back if driving

## PENISCOLA

**Distance:** ~150 km north of Valencia (Castellon province) | **Time needed:** Full day | **Best for:** Medieval history, coastal scenery, Game of Thrones fans, seafood

### Why Peniscola?

Peniscola is one of the most visually striking towns on Spain's Mediterranean coast. Known as the **"City in the Sea"**, its walled old town rises dramatically from a rocky promontory jutting into the sea, crowned by the imposing **Castle of Papa Luna** -- the last Templar fortress in Spain, which later served as the seat of a rival papacy during the Western Schism.

Within the medieval walls, narrow cobblestone streets wind between whitewashed houses with flower-filled balconies, tumbling down toward the shoreline. The castle, built by the **Knights Templar** in the late 13th-early 14th century, later became the residence of **Pope Benedict XIII** (Pedro de Luna, known as "Papa Luna"), who refused to resign even when the schism was resolved. He lived here from 1411 until his death in 1423, effectively making Peniscola the seat of a rival papacy.

**Game of Thrones Connection:** Peniscola's medieval walls, streets, and castle were used as a filming location for HBO's Game of Thrones in **Season 6**, serving as the setting for **Meereen** (the city ruled by Daenerys Targaryen). There are explanatory markers at the filming locations throughout the old town.

In late March, the town will be pleasantly quiet compared to summer. The castle follows winter hours (10:30-17:30) but everything will be open and accessible. The weather should be mild enough for a promenade along the beach.

### All Attractions

#### Papa Luna Castle (Castillo de Peniscola) -- MUST SEE

The crown jewel. Built by the Knights Templar in the late 13th-early 14th century, it is the last Templar fortress in Spain. Later served as the papal residence of Benedict XIII (Papa Luna) during the Western Schism from 1411 until his death in 1423. Features videos, artifacts, and period furnishings explaining life during both the Templar and papal eras. Stunning panoramic views of the Mediterranean from the ramparts.

- **Hours (March = winter):** 10:30 - 17:30 (ticket office closes at 17:00)
- **Closed:** January 1 & 6, September 9, October 9, December 25
- **Prices:** General 5 EUR | Reduced 3.50 EUR (students, teachers, unemployed, groups 30+, ages 9-16, seniors 60+) | Free for children under 8, disabled persons, Peniscola residents
- **Payment:** Card only (no cash accepted)
- **Rules:** No food, drink, smoking, pets, swimwear, or scooters inside
- **Allow:** 1-1.5 hours for a thorough visit
- **Official site:** https://castillodepeniscola.dipcas.es/en/

#### Artillery Park (Parc d'Artilleria)

A 16th-century military redoubt transformed into a garden. Features historical tunnels, batteries, powder magazines, cannons, bunkers, and panoramic sea views. The contrast of flowers growing among fortification ruins is striking.

- **Included** in the castle admission ticket
- Adjacent to the castle

#### El Bufador (The Blowhole)

A natural rock formation next to the castle, 7 meters high, formed by natural erosion with a large hole at the bottom. When waves crash through, it creates dramatic spray and a distinctive "snorting" sound (bufar = to snort in Valencian). Also houses the image of the city's patron saint.

- **Best viewed at high tide** for the most dramatic effect
- **Free** to visit
- Located along the rocks near the castle base -- follow signs

#### Casa de las Petxines (House of Shells)

A distinctive facade covered with thousands of seashells, creating a glittering effect. Very photogenic. Located near the 19th-century lighthouse.

- **Free** to view (exterior only)

#### Old Town Walls & Gates

- **Porta de Sant Pere** (15th century) -- the main entrance to the old town from the harbor side
- **Portal Fosc** (16th century) -- the "Dark Gate" at the opposite end
- Three churches within the walls: **Santa Maria** (with tower), **La Ermitana**, and **Santa Ana** hermitage

#### Museum of the Sea (Museu de la Mar)

Small maritime museum along the sea wall on Mayor street. Features historical cannons and maritime artifacts. A quick but interesting stop.

- **Allow:** 20-30 minutes

#### Magic Museum by Yunke

A quirky museum of magic and illusion inside the old town. Fascinating for all ages. Features a mysterious world of magic tricks and illusions.

#### Beaches

- **North Beach (Playa Norte):** The most famous -- over 5 km of fine golden sand stretching toward Benicarlo. Calm waters, great for a post-old-town stroll. Even in March, a walk along the promenade is lovely.
- **South Beach:** Smaller, closer to the old town
- **Sierra de Irta Natural Park coves** (south of town): Cala Puerto Negro, Cala del Moro, Playa del Ruso, Cala Argilaga -- wild, scenic coves requiring a car or significant walking. For serious nature lovers.

### Walking Route Through the Old Town (2-3 hours)

1. **Start at Porta de Sant Pere** (15th-century gate) -- the main entrance from the harbor/port area
2. Walk uphill through narrow medieval streets. Follow signs to the castle but don't rush -- wander the side alleys
3. Admire whitewashed houses with colorful balconies and flower pots
4. Find **El Bufador** (blowhole) -- look for the path along the rocks near the castle base. Best at high tide.
5. Enter **Papa Luna Castle** -- spend 1-1.5 hours exploring the Templar/papal history and enjoying the panoramic views from the ramparts (opens 10:30, 5 EUR)
6. Exit the castle and explore the **Artillery Park** (included in ticket)
7. Walk to **Casa de las Petxines** (House of Shells) and the nearby **19th-century lighthouse** -- great photo spot
8. Stroll down **Calle Mayor** (Mayor street) -- the main artery of the old town, lined with small shops and cafes
9. Visit the **Museum of the Sea** along the way
10. Look for **Game of Thrones filming location markers** throughout -- they have explanatory panels
11. Peek into the three churches: Santa Maria, La Ermitana, Santa Ana
12. Exit through **Portal Fosc** (16th-century "Dark Gate")
13. Descend to the port area and walk along the **North Beach promenade**

**Total walking time:** 2-3 hours at a leisurely pace, not counting time inside the castle or lunch.

### Where to Eat

Peniscola is a fishing town -- the seafood is excellent and very fresh.

#### What to Order
- **Arroz a banda** -- rice cooked in rich fish stock (a Peniscola specialty)
- **Arroz con bogavante** -- lobster rice (splurge dish)
- **Fideuada** -- noodle paella, a coastal Castellon classic
- **Cazuela de rape** -- monkfish casserole
- **Calamari, razor clams, coquinas** -- fresh seafood tapas
- **Paella** -- this is Valencia region, after all

#### Restaurant Recommendations

**In/Near the Old Town:**
- **El Chiringuito de Pepe:** Old town classic, famous for "croqueton" (giant croquettes) and rice dishes. Nautical decor, terrace, relaxed vibe.
- **El Penon:** Traditional Mediterranean with great seafood. Stone-wall decor, terrace dining.
- **Al Masset:** Bar adjacent to the castle with Mediterranean views -- perfect for a drink and light bite while overlooking the sea.
- **Bar La Pulperia:** Specializes in fresh octopus. Also pinchos, tapas, calamari with garlic, razor clams, coquinas.

**Budget-Friendly:**
- **La Frescura:** Small restaurant on the beach with a terrace. Possibly the best quality-price ratio in town. Good paella ~15 EUR.
- **Menu del dia** at various restaurants: typically 12-15 EUR for a full 3-course meal (soup/salad + main + dessert + drink). Look for signs outside restaurants.
- **El Pescador Ermitano:** Promenade spot for tapas and paella at reasonable prices.

**On the Promenade/Beach:**
- **Taberna La Copla:** Paella and traditional cuisine
- **Boca:** Terrace bar with cocktails and scenic views
- **Tio Pepe:** Valencian dishes with a modern twist, mostly seafood-based, reasonably priced

> **Budget tip:** The menu del dia is your friend. For 12-15 EUR you get a full meal with drink. Available at most restaurants during lunch hours (13:30-15:30). Even the seafood-focused places often have one.

### Nearby Combinations

#### Benicarlo (7-8 km north) -- Easy to Combine

- Coastal hiking trails with wild coves and beaches

- UNESCO-recognized underground river cave system -- you take a boat through the caves
